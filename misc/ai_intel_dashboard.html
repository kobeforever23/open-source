Section 02 // Frontier Research

TOP WHITEPAPERS & FOUNDATIONAL RESEARCH

The papers that actually matter — curated by impact on production AI systems, not citation count. Organized by three tiers: Foundational (must-know), Architectural (defines how systems are built), and Applied (directly relevant to CIB risk functions). These are the intellectual building blocks of every AI system being deployed in capital markets today.

TIER 1 — FOUNDATIONAL (THE CANON)
"Attention Is All You Need" — Vaswani et al. (Google Brain, 2017)
VENUE: NeurIPS 2017 // CITATIONS: 100,000+ // STATUS: THE PAPER THAT STARTED EVERYTHING
Introduced the Transformer architecture — the foundation of every major language model in existence today. Replaced recurrent neural networks with a purely attention-based mechanism, enabling massively parallel training and the scaling laws that make GPT-4 and Claude possible. Understanding attention heads and positional encoding is understanding why LLMs can hold complex multi-document context without "forgetting."
WHY IT MATTERS FOR RISK: Every AI tool you evaluate — Claude, GPT, Gemini — runs on this architecture. Understanding attention mechanisms explains both the power (long-range dependencies across a 200K token risk report) and the failure modes (attention dilution, positional bias) of every LLM in production.
"Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models" — Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020)
VENUE: OpenAI Technical Report // IMPACT: Defined the investment thesis for all frontier model development
Established empirically that model performance scales predictably with compute, parameters, and data — following power laws. This paper is why every major tech company spent the last 5 years in a compute arms race: more FLOPS = better model, reliably, across domains. The "bitter lesson" of AI: scale beats clever engineering.
WHY IT MATTERS: Explains why DeepSeek's $6M training efficiency is a genuine inflection point — it suggests we may have been over-parameterizing frontier models, and that smarter architectures can achieve capability gains without proportional compute cost. This has major implications for on-prem deployment economics in banking.
"Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback" — Bai et al. (Anthropic, 2022)
VENUE: Anthropic Technical Report // RELEVANCE: Defines the safety/reliability framework for enterprise AI
Introduced Constitutional AI (CAI) — training AI models to be helpful, harmless, and honest using a set of principles rather than purely human feedback. Models are trained to evaluate and revise their own outputs against a constitution of principles. This framework is why Claude models exhibit the consistent, governance-friendly behavior that makes them suitable for regulated environments.
WHY IT MATTERS FOR RISK: When deploying AI in model-risk-sensitive environments, the consistency and predictability of behavior matters as much as capability. CAI explains why Claude is less likely to hallucinate confidently in risk scenarios — and why it's the preferred choice for Goldman Sachs and other banks that need auditable AI behavior.
"DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning" — DeepSeek-AI (2025)
VENUE: arXiv 2025 // IMPACT: Destabilized frontier pricing; open-sourced reasoning capability
Demonstrated that a reasoning model matching OpenAI o1 performance could be trained for an estimated $6M using reinforcement learning on chain-of-thought traces — dramatically below the cost structure of Western frontier labs. Released open-source weights. The immediate market reaction: Nvidia lost $590B in market cap in a single day as investors repriced AI compute demand assumptions.
WHY IT MATTERS: The strategic implication is that AI capability is commoditizing faster than expected. The moat is no longer "access to frontier models" — it's proprietary data, domain workflow integration, and institutional deployment know-how. For Wells Fargo: the build vs. buy calculus shifted. Open-source reasoning models deployable on-prem eliminate data residency risk while approaching frontier capability.
TIER 2 — ARCHITECTURAL (HOW PRODUCTION SYSTEMS ARE BUILT)
"ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models" — Yao et al. (Princeton/Google, 2022)
VENUE: ICLR 2023 // CITATION TRAJECTORY: Foundational for all agent frameworks
Introduced the ReAct paradigm — interleaving reasoning traces (thinking) with action execution (tool use, search, code execution). The model alternates between "thought" and "act" steps, using external tools to gather information before formulating a response. This is the direct ancestor of every modern AI agent framework including LangGraph, AutoGen, and Claude's tool use.
WHY IT MATTERS: ReAct is why AI agents can browse the web, execute Python, query databases, and call APIs — and do so with coherent reasoning rather than random action sequences. Every production agent workflow you'll encounter is a descendant of this paper.
"Toolformer: Language Models Can Teach Themselves to Use Tools" — Schick et al. (Meta AI, 2023)
VENUE: NeurIPS 2023 // IMPACT: Validated self-supervised tool learning at scale
Demonstrated that LLMs can learn to use external tools (calculators, search APIs, translation systems) in a self-supervised way — generating their own training data for tool use. Key finding: a 7B parameter model with tools outperforms a 175B parameter model without them on many tasks. The implication: tool access is worth more than raw parameters for complex real-world tasks.
WHY IT MATTERS FOR RISK: This is the theoretical basis for why an AI agent with access to your risk data systems, calculation engines, and regulatory databases can outperform a much larger standalone model. Architecture > raw capability for domain-specific deployment.
"RAG: Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks" — Lewis et al. (Facebook AI, 2020)
VENUE: NeurIPS 2020 // ADOPTION: Every major enterprise AI deployment uses this architecture
Introduced RAG — combining a parametric language model with a non-parametric memory (document retrieval). The model retrieves relevant documents at inference time and conditions its response on them. This allows LLMs to reason over private, proprietary, or recently updated information without retraining — solving the knowledge cutoff and data privacy problems simultaneously.
WHY IT MATTERS: RAG is the architecture that makes AI useful for institutional knowledge work. The ability to query an LLM over your CCAR documentation, internal stress test history, regulatory correspondence, and risk policies — and get accurate, sourced responses — depends entirely on RAG. This is the most important architectural concept for enterprise AI deployment in banking.
"Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior" — Park et al. (Stanford, 2023)
VENUE: UIST 2023 // IMPACT: First demonstration of persistent, believable AI agent societies
Created 25 AI agents with distinct identities, memories, and goals in a simulated town. Agents formed opinions, spread information, made plans, and exhibited emergent social behavior. Introduced three key components now standard in agent architectures: memory streams, reflection synthesis, and plan generation. First rigorous demonstration that LLMs can sustain coherent autonomous behavior over extended time horizons.
WHY IT MATTERS: This paper established the cognitive architecture that modern enterprise agents are built on — memory, reflection, and planning. It explains why well-designed AI agents don't lose context across a multi-day risk analysis workflow, and how multi-agent systems can coordinate without explicit human orchestration at every step.
"AutoGen: Enabling Next-Gen LLM Applications via Multi-Agent Conversation" — Wu et al. (Microsoft, 2023)
VENUE: arXiv 2023 / ICLR 2024 // ADOPTION: 30K+ GitHub stars; production deployments at Microsoft
Introduced AutoGen — a framework for building multi-agent systems where LLMs communicate with each other to solve complex tasks. Agents have roles (coder, reviewer, executor, critic), exchange messages, and collectively produce outputs neither could achieve alone. The paper demonstrated that multi-agent conversation significantly outperforms single-agent approaches on complex coding, math, and reasoning tasks.
WHY IT MATTERS: AutoGen is one of the two most important open-source agent frameworks (alongside LangGraph) for building production CIB risk AI systems. Its multi-agent conversation model maps directly to how risk teams actually work: analyst drafts scenario → reviewer checks cross-asset consistency → validator checks SR 11-7 compliance → documentation is produced. See Tab 4 for full framework comparison.
TIER 3 — APPLIED (DIRECTLY RELEVANT TO CIB RISK)
"Are Large Language Models a Threat to Financial Analysts?" — Lopez-Lira et al. (2023)
VENUE: SSRN 2023 // SCOPE: Largest empirical study of LLM vs. analyst performance on financial tasks
Empirical study comparing GPT-4 performance against professional equity analysts on earnings forecasting, financial statement analysis, and risk assessment tasks. Findings: LLMs outperformed analysts on structured data tasks (ratio analysis, comparative benchmarking) and matched them on qualitative narrative tasks, but underperformed on novel market structure analysis requiring deep domain intuition. The performance gap correlated with task structuredness — the more structured the task, the larger the LLM advantage.
WHY IT MATTERS: This paper maps the displacement frontier. Structured, repeatable analytical tasks — the majority of BAU risk analytics work — are AI-favorable. Unstructured, novel, judgment-intensive tasks (reading market microstructure, novel geopolitical risk) remain human-favorable. The implication for staffing: the boundary between these categories is moving toward human-favorable every 12 months.
"FinGPT: Open-Source Financial Large Language Models" — Yang et al. (Columbia / AI4Finance, 2023)
VENUE: arXiv 2023 // RELEVANCE: Practical framework for domain-specific LLM fine-tuning in finance
Introduced FinGPT — an open-source framework for fine-tuning LLMs on financial data including news, filings, earnings calls, and market data. Demonstrated that lightweight fine-tuning (LoRA) on domain-specific data significantly improves LLM performance on financial tasks vs. general-purpose models, at a fraction of the cost of full fine-tuning. Includes data pipeline architecture for continuous model updating from real-time financial feeds.
WHY IT MATTERS: Provides the technical blueprint for building a Wells Fargo-specific risk analytics LLM — fine-tuned on internal documentation, historical stress results, regulatory correspondence — that outperforms general models on CIB-specific tasks. This is the academic foundation for the "build over buy" argument in risk AI.
"BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance" — Wu et al. (Bloomberg, 2023)
VENUE: arXiv 2023 // SIGNIFICANCE: First financial domain LLM from a major data provider
Bloomberg trained a 50B parameter LLM on 363B tokens of financial data (Bloomberg's proprietary dataset) plus general text. BloombergGPT outperformed comparably-sized general models on financial tasks (NLP classification, sentiment analysis, named entity recognition in financial documents) while maintaining competitive general performance. Validates the "domain-specialized model" hypothesis.
WHY IT MATTERS: Proof of concept that a financial institution with proprietary data can build a domain-specialized model that outperforms general-purpose frontier models on financial tasks. The lesson is not "Bloomberg did it" — it's that proprietary financial language data is a training asset that general models can't replicate. Whoever builds the first CIB-risk-specialized model wins a durable internal competitive advantage.
"Language Agents as Digital Twins for Finance" — Zhang et al. (2024)
VENUE: NeurIPS Workshop 2024 // RELEVANCE: Directly maps agent architecture to financial risk workflows
Proposed and demonstrated a framework using LLM-based agents as "digital twins" of human financial analysts — capable of processing news, earnings data, and market signals to generate investment and risk assessments. Demonstrated that properly structured agent workflows (with memory, tool use, and reflection) could replicate 70–80% of a junior analyst's daily output at negligible marginal cost, with measurable accuracy on backtested financial decisions.
WHY IT MATTERS: The "digital twin" framing is the most useful mental model for institutional AI deployment. Don't think of AI as a tool — think of it as a parallel analyst workforce that needs the same information access, workflow structure, and quality control that human analysts receive. The implication: AI deployment is an org design problem as much as a technology problem.
Section 03 // Agentic AI

THE AGENTIC AI LANDSCAPE

The shift from LLM-as-tool to LLM-as-autonomous-agent is the most consequential architectural change in enterprise AI since the Transformer itself. This section covers the full landscape: what agents are, who's building the infrastructure, how enterprise platforms compare, and why the agentic paradigm changes the workforce equation fundamentally.

What "Agentic AI" Actually Means: An AI agent is a system where an LLM takes a high-level goal and autonomously decomposes it into sub-tasks, selects tools, executes actions, observes results, and iterates — without requiring human input at each step. The defining characteristics are: goal-directedness (acts toward an objective), tool use (can interact with external systems), memory (maintains context over time), and autonomy (makes its own decisions within a defined scope). The paradigm shift: from "LLM responds to prompts" to "LLM pursues objectives."

It integrates natively with Google Workspace, Salesforce, ServiceNow, Jira, and major data platforms — and runs on Gemini 2.0 with 1M token context.

Core architecture: Agentspace uses a "multi-agent" model: a central orchestrator agent routes tasks to specialized sub-agents (e.g., a document analysis agent, a code execution agent, a data query agent) — coordinating complex multi-step workflows that span multiple enterprise systems.

NotebookLM integration: NotebookLM — Google's AI knowledge synthesis tool — is embedded in Agentspace, allowing agents to ingest and reason over entire document corpora (risk policies, CCAR documentation, regulatory guidance) without external data exposure.

Section 04 // Multi-Agent Architecture

MULTI-AGENT FRAMEWORK DEEP DIVE

Understanding multi-agent systems is the single most important architectural concept for AI deployment in CIB risk analytics. This section explains the patterns, the building blocks, the current best implementations, and how to think about designing agent workflows for institutional risk functions.

Why Multi-Agent Over Single Agent: Complex institutional workflows — stress scenario generation, regulatory submission preparation, limit breach triage — require multiple distinct capabilities: data retrieval, quantitative calculation, qualitative reasoning, compliance checking, documentation generation, approval routing. No single LLM call does all of these well simultaneously. Multi-agent systems decompose the problem: each agent specializes in one capability, and an orchestrator coordinates them. The result is higher quality, more auditable, and more reliable than asking one LLM to do everything at once.

THE CORE AGENT ARCHITECTURE PATTERNS
PATTERN 01Orchestrator → Subagent (Hierarchical)

Structure: A central "orchestrator" LLM receives a high-level goal, decomposes it into tasks, delegates each task to a specialized subagent, synthesizes the results, and produces a final output. The orchestrator doesn't execute tasks — it routes, coordinates, and integrates.

Risk analytics example: Orchestrator receives "generate AI bubble correction stress scenario." Delegates to: (1) Data Agent — pull current market levels; (2) Shock Calibration Agent — derive cross-asset shocks using historical analogs; (3) Consistency Agent — check internal shock logic; (4) Narrative Agent — produce regulatory-grade written scenario. Orchestrator synthesizes all outputs.

Best framework: LangGraph (explicit graph edges), Google ADK (hierarchical agents), CrewAI (manager crew)

PATTERN 02Peer Collaboration (Debate / Review)

Structure: Multiple agents with different "perspectives" or roles tackle the same problem and critique each other's outputs. A "generator" agent produces work; a "critic" agent evaluates it; a "resolver" agent integrates the critique. Can iterate multiple rounds. Research shows this significantly outperforms single-agent output on reasoning-intensive tasks.

Risk analytics example: Scenario Generator Agent proposes stress shocks → Cross-Asset Consistency Critic Agent evaluates whether equity, rates, FX, and credit shocks are internally consistent → SR 11-7 Compliance Agent checks documentation language → Resolver synthesizes final scenario. Each "round" of debate improves quality.

Best framework: AutoGen (native multi-agent conversation), custom LangGraph implementations

PATTERN 03Plan-Execute (Reasoning + Action)

Structure: Agent first generates a detailed plan for completing a task (using a reasoning model like o3 or Claude ET), then executes each step, observing results and re-planning as needed. The planning and execution phases may use different models — expensive reasoning model for planning, fast efficient model for execution.

Risk analytics example: Human asks for "comprehensive VaR attribution for fixed income desks this week." Agent plans: what data to pull, what calculations to run, what format to produce, what review steps are needed. Executes each step against risk data systems. Re-plans if data is incomplete or calculations surface anomalies requiring investigation.

Best framework: OpenAI Responses API + o3 for planning; LangGraph for execution graph; Anthropic for auditable reasoning

PATTERN 04Event-Driven (Reactive Agent Networks)

Structure: Agents are triggered by events — a market data signal, a limit breach, a news item, a regulatory filing — and autonomously initiate analysis and alert workflows without human prompting. Events flow through a message bus; agents subscribe to relevant event types and respond. Persistent background agents that never sleep.

Risk analytics example: Market data feed detects SPX moves -3% intraday → Triggers Scenario Assessment Agent → Agent computes implied stress impact across all 19 desks → Generates preliminary breach risk report → Alerts risk officer if any desk shows >20% VaR limit utilization spike → Prepends historical context from past similar events via RAG.

Best framework: Kafka/Redis event streaming + LangGraph agents; Amazon Bedrock event agents; Azure Event Grid + Copilot agents

AGENT MEMORY ARCHITECTURE — THE CRITICAL DESIGN ELEMENT
IN-CONTEXT (Working Memory)

Everything in the current LLM context window. Ephemeral — disappears when the session ends. For frontier models: 200K tokens (Claude) or 1M tokens (Gemini) — enough to hold entire workflows, documents, and data in working memory simultaneously. The limiting factor is cost and latency at maximum context length.

Use: current task context, immediate conversation history, document under analysis
EXTERNAL VECTOR (Long-Term Memory)

Embeddings stored in a vector database (Pinecone, Weaviate, pgvector). Retrieved at runtime via semantic similarity search and injected into context. This is the RAG layer — allows agents to "remember" across sessions, access institutional knowledge, and reason over large corpora that exceed context window limits.

Use: historical stress scenarios, risk policies, regulatory guidance, past CCAR submissions
STRUCTURED (State Memory)

Traditional databases and key-value stores that agents read and write to. Provides deterministic, queryable state — critical for tracking workflow progress, storing intermediate results, maintaining audit logs, and enabling human review checkpoints. LangGraph's state graph is a structured memory implementation purpose-built for agent workflows.

Use: workflow state, approval status, calculation results, risk limits, audit trail
SKILLS & TOOL USE — THE AGENT'S CAPABILITY SURFACE

An agent's capability is determined by its tool set — the external systems it can interact with. Tool design is the most important architectural decision in agent deployment. Well-designed tools produce consistent, parseable outputs; poorly designed tools cause agent failure cascades. For CIB risk, the critical tool categories are:

DATA RETRIEVAL TOOLS
Market data APIs (Bloomberg, Refinitiv), internal risk data warehouse queries (SQL), VaR engine calls, position downloads, limit table reads. These are the "eyes" of the risk agent — without accurate, real-time data access, all downstream analysis is garbage-in.
COMPUTATION TOOLS
Python execution environments (sandbox), financial calculation libraries (QuantLib, NumPy), scenario engine calls, Monte Carlo simulation runners. Critical design rule: agents should never do arithmetic themselves — always delegate to code execution with verifiable results.
KNOWLEDGE RETRIEVAL (RAG)
Vector search over institutional documentation: risk policies, historical scenarios, regulatory guidance, CCAR submissions, desk-level limit documentation. The quality of the RAG corpus determines the quality of agent knowledge — this is the most important long-term investment in agent infrastructure.
COMMUNICATION TOOLS
Email/Teams APIs for routing alerts and reports, document generation (Word, PDF, HTML), ticket creation in workflow systems, Slack/Teams notifications. These are the "hands" — agents that can analyze but can't communicate or route are limited to human-initiated workflows only.
EXTERNAL INTELLIGENCE
Web search, news APIs, regulatory publication feeds (FDIC, OCC, Fed), earnings call transcripts, regulatory filing parsers (SEC EDGAR). Allows agents to stay current without human research cycles — critical for geopolitical risk monitoring and regulatory change management.
HUMAN-IN-LOOP TOOLS
Approval request interfaces, escalation routing, uncertainty flags, confidence scoring. The most important governance tools — allow agents to stop, report state, and request human judgment at defined decision points. Non-negotiable for SR 11-7 compliant agent deployment in capital decisions.

The Governance Design Imperative: Every agentic workflow deployed in a risk function requires explicit answers to four questions before production: (1) What decisions can the agent make autonomously? (2) What triggers a mandatory human checkpoint? (3) What is the full audit trail of agent actions? (4) How is agent failure detected and recovered? Systems that don't answer these questions explicitly are not production-ready for capital-sensitive workflows regardless of capability. This is the governance wrapper that distinguishes an AI toy from an institutional tool.

Section 06 // Human Capital Strategy

HUMAN CAPITAL STRATEGY FOR SENIOR LEADERS

For a Head of Risk Analytics navigating the AI transition, the human capital question is the most consequential and politically sensitive one they will face. This section provides a strategic framework for thinking about how to position the function, how to respond to senior management pressure on headcount, and how to build the AI-augmented team architecture of 2027–2028.

The Uncomfortable Reality: If a senior leader in risk analytics deploys AI aggressively, they will be able to demonstrate — in concrete, documented terms — that 30–50% of their current junior analyst headcount could be replaced with AI agents on structured analytical tasks within 18–24 months. Senior management will see those numbers. The question is not whether this conversation happens, but who frames it first and how. The leader who proactively shapes the narrative controls the outcome; the leader who waits to be asked is responding to someone else's framing.

STRATEGIC POSITIONS AVAILABLE TO A HEAD OF RISK ANALYTICS
POSITION A — CONTROLLED TRANSFORMATIONThe Proactive Architect

The stance: "I am building the AI-augmented risk function of the future. I'm leading this transformation, defining the governance, and controlling the timeline. Headcount decisions will be mine to make, in service of the business, with my team retrained and repositioned — not imposed on me from outside."

What this requires: A clear AI deployment roadmap with 6/12/24-month milestones. An honest capability assessment of current staff — who can grow into AI-adjacent roles, who can't. A reskilling program that moves high-potential analysts toward AI oversight, model governance, and strategic risk roles. Demonstrated productivity gains that justify the investment in AI infrastructure.

The advantage: You control the narrative, protect your best people, and emerge as the AI transformation leader in CIB. The most coveted senior risk roles in 2027–2028 will be people who successfully ran this playbook.

POSITION B — REACTIVE DEFENSEThe Resistor

The stance: "The risk function requires human judgment, regulatory scrutiny of AI is real, and we should be cautious about deploying unproven technology in capital-sensitive workflows."

Why this fails: Every true statement in this position is also a temporary one. The regulatory scrutiny argument weakens as governance frameworks mature. The "unproven" argument weakens as JPM and GS publish deployment results. The human judgment argument is correct for 20% of the work — but someone will note it doesn't apply to the other 80%. The leader who resists loses the narrative to the CFO or COO who sees the productivity numbers and asks the question themselves — except in that scenario, the head of risk doesn't shape the answer.

The risk: Being bypassed. A technologically aggressive CFO or COO who sees AI productivity numbers — at peers or in published research — will eventually ask why the risk function hasn't captured equivalent value. Not having an answer is a career-defining moment.

CRITICAL UNCERTAINTIES — THE VARIABLES THAT DETERMINE WHICH SCENARIO MATERIALIZES
UNCERTAINTY 01Reasoning Model Capability Trajectory

The question: Do reasoning models (o3, o4+, Claude 5+) achieve reliable human-expert performance on unstructured financial judgment tasks in the 2026–2028 window, or do they plateau at structured-task automation?

Why it matters: If models plateau at structured tasks, the human moat (strategic judgment, novel scenario design, regulatory relationships) remains intact. If models break through to unstructured judgment, the displacement timeline compresses significantly and the skills that protect senior risk professionals change fundamentally.

Current signal: Reasoning models are progressing faster than expected. O3's ARC-AGI score suggests novel problem-solving capability approaching human performance. The realistic horizon for structured financial analysis automation is 12–18 months, not 36+.

UNCERTAINTY 02Regulatory Response to AI in Risk Functions

The question: Does the Fed/OCC publish prescriptive guidance on AI use in capital-adjacent risk workflows within 12–18 months, and if so, does it enable or constrain deployment?

Why it matters: Prescriptive guidance that creates a clear compliance pathway enables aggressive deployment (Scenario A/B). Prescriptive guidance that requires full model validation for every AI touchpoint near capital decisions significantly slows the timeline (Scenario C).

Current signal: Draft SR 11-7 LLM addendum is circulating. Early indications suggest a tiered framework — higher scrutiny for decision-proximate AI, lighter framework for information/support AI. This is the best outcome: enables deployment with governance, rather than blocking it.

UNCERTAINTY 03Open-Source Model Quality Curve

The question: Do open-source models (Llama 4, Mistral, DeepSeek successors) reach frontier-equivalent quality at a pace that makes on-premises deployment the preferred option for data-sensitive banking workflows?

Why it matters: If on-prem open-source reaches frontier quality, the data residency barrier to AI deployment in banking largely disappears. Every workflow involving sensitive position data, client information, or proprietary risk models can be automated without API data exposure. The build vs. buy decision also shifts dramatically — infrastructure cost replaces per-token cost.

Current signal: DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated reasoning-class performance at dramatically reduced compute cost, suggesting the gap between open-source and frontier is closing faster than most expected 12 months ago.

UNCERTAINTY 04Institutional Governance Maturity Speed

The question: Do financial institutions develop AI governance frameworks fast enough to enable aggressive deployment, or does governance lag create a capability gap that competitors fill?

Why it matters: AI capabilities are advancing faster than institutional governance frameworks. Banks that develop governance infrastructure quickly will deploy aggressively and safely. Banks where governance development lags will either deploy recklessly (regulatory risk) or not at all (competitive risk). The governance function is the strategic bottleneck.

Current signal: JPM and GS are 18–24 months ahead of most peers on governance infrastructure, having deployed production AI systems and iterated governance frameworks based on real operational experience. The gap between governance leaders and laggards is growing, not closing.

The people who can design agent workflows, validate AI outputs, and govern AI deployment are already scarce. The external market for this talent will only tighten. Identify and accelerate development of the 2–3 people in your function who are genuinely AI-fluent today.
4. RUN CONTROLLED PILOTS WITH DOCUMENTED RESULTS
Evidence-based deployment decisions require empirical evidence. A pilot that fails teaches you as much as one that succeeds. Build the institutional learning infrastructure now.

WATCH AND DECIDE — SCENARIO-DEPENDENT

A. HEADCOUNT DECISIONS
Defer structural headcount decisions until pilot productivity evidence is institutional, not theoretical. Do not promise headcount reduction until you have empirical data from your specific workflows and data environment.
B. FULL AGENTIC PRODUCTION DEPLOYMENT
Scale cautiously from pilot to production. The failure modes in production are different from controlled testing — particularly in risk functions where downstream consumers depend on output accuracy. Human oversight at production launch is non-negotiable.
C. BUILD VS. BUY FINAL DECISION
The optimal decision depends on open-source model trajectory, regulatory environment, and Tachyon's actual capability delivery. Make this decision with 12-month evidence, not today's assumptions.
