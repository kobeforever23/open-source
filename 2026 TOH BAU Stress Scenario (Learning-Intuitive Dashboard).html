How to read any stress scenario

This is the shortest learning path from jargon to intuition.

1. Start with the movie

The movie is the causal chain: inflation cools, growth cracks, liquidity dries up, or inflation stays sticky. Do not start with a spreadsheet.

2. Check the regime fingerprint

If the movie is true, rates, spreads, equities, oil, the DXY, and funding measures should move in a way that hangs together.

3. Ask who bleeds first

Losses usually show up first in the books with the largest sensitivity, weakest liquidity, or nastiest optionality.

4. Ask what people often get wrong

The best learning happens where intuition fails: lower rates can still hurt, and a short shock can be worse than a long one if plumbing breaks.

Myths to kill early

These are the beginner mistakes that make scenario discussions confusing.

"Rates down is always good."

No. Lower yields can hurt NII, carry, and mortgage optionality even while government bonds rally.

"Spread widening only comes from defaults."

No. Spreads can gap wider because of liquidity, positioning, forced selling, or a higher risk premium before defaults visibly rise.

"Flight to quality equals recession."

No. A flight to quality is often about speed and market plumbing. Macro data can look fine while P&L is already bleeding.

"Severe just means Moderate with bigger numbers."

No. A strong scenario set changes the regime logic too: policy response, market functioning, optionality, and cross-asset relationships can all shift.

Advanced note: why governance packets often use ranges instead of one exact number

Ranges are useful because they acknowledge calibration uncertainty and portfolio heterogeneity. They force the reviewer to focus on regime logic and relative severity, not on false precision.

Common beginner mistakes

These are worth memorizing because they show up in real meetings.

Confusing level and direction

High rates and falling rates are not the same as low rates and falling rates. The starting point matters for business pain.

Ignoring starting valuations

Tight spreads, rich equities, and low implied liquidity buffers make a moderate downturn more dangerous than the label sounds.

Forgetting optionality

A book can have small linear risk and still blow out under large moves because the hedge profile changes.

Using macro words without book translation

If you cannot say which desk or sensitivity gets hit, you do not yet understand the scenario in business terms.
