The Pre-Mortem
Used by Bezos and Dalio — imagine the decision has failed, then work backwards to find the real risks before they happen.
I'm about to make this decision: [DESCRIBE YOUR DECISION] Imagine it is 12 months from now and this decision has been a clear failure. Write a detailed post-mortem explaining: 1. What went wrong (give 3 specific, realistic failure scenarios) 2. What early warning signs we missed 3. Which stakeholder objections we should have taken more seriously 4. What external factors shifted that we didn't anticipate 5. What we would do differently with hindsight Then flip it: Given this pre-mortem analysis, what specific actions should I take NOW — before committing — to reduce the probability of these failure modes? Be concrete and prioritized.

Red-Team Any Decision
🧠 30 minCopilotAlt: Claude
Forces you to confront the strongest arguments against your position before committing.
Search my recent emails and Teams conversations about [DECISION TOPIC] to gather the latest context. I am the CEO of a large multinational and I'm about to make the following decision: [DESCRIBE YOUR DECISION IN 2-3 SENTENCES] Using any relevant context from my recent communications, argue AGAINST this decision with maximum intellectual rigor: 1. The 3 strongest arguments against proceeding — each one strong enough to give me genuine pause 2. The hidden assumptions I'm making that I probably don't even realize (cognitive blind spots) 3. The worst PROBABLE outcome — not a tail risk, the realistic downside that happens 20-30% of the time with decisions like this 4. The stakeholder who will oppose this most fiercely (board members, regulators, investors, employees, clients, partners) — articulate their strongest argument in their voice 5. The information that, if available, would change everything — and whether I can get it before committing 6. The second-order effects I'm underweighting — what happens after the initial impact plays out? Do NOT hedge. Be direct and forceful. I need adversarial thinking, not diplomacy. After making your case: Rate the decision 1-10 for risk-adjusted merit, and tell me the ONE change to the decision design that would move it 2 points higher.

Board Memo — Recommendation-First Format
🔥 10 minCopilotAlt: Claude
Uses the Amazon-style recommendation-first format that top boards actually want — not the slow build-up they sit through.
Pull from my recent documents about [TOPIC] and any relevant data shared in my Teams channels. Write a board memo using the recommendation-first structure used at top-performing multinationals (Amazon 6-pager discipline, Berkshire conciseness). Organization type: [INDUSTRY, SCALE — e.g., "global bank, $500B assets" or "Fortune 200 technology company"] Structure: 1. RECOMMENDATION (1-2 sentences — the specific ask with a deadline. A board member reading ONLY this sentence knows what to decide.) 2. WHY NOW (3-4 sentences — the forcing function. What changed that makes this the right moment?) 3. KEY FACTS (5-7 bullets — data that drives the recommendation. Each bullet contains a specific number, date, or verifiable claim.) 4. RISKS & MITIGANTS (table: risk | probability | impact | mitigant | owner) 5. FINANCIAL IMPACT (quantified: revenue/cost/capital impact with confidence intervals. NPV where applicable.) 6. ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED (2-3 alternatives with 1-sentence explanation of why each was rejected) 7. REQUESTED ACTION (specific: "Approve X by [date], authorize management to Y, with Z as a gating condition") Rules: 1.5-2 pages maximum. Every sentence passes the "so what?" test. No hedging language. Board members should be able to read sections 1 and 7 only and understand the full ask.

Cheat Sheet — Executive Reference Card

10 Prompting Principles That Actually Matter

1. Give it a role before giving it a task

"You are a senior risk advisor with 30 years in financial services" produces dramatically better output than "Help me think about risk." The role constrains knowledge and tone to what you need.

2. State your desired output format explicitly

Instead of "analyze this," say "Provide: 1) A 3-sentence summary 2) Top 5 risks in table format 3) Recommended action." Format determines usefulness.

3. Provide the "so what" context — who is the audience?

"Write this as if it will be read by the Board of Directors" produces entirely different output than "write a summary." The audience determines depth, tone, and emphasis.

4. Ask it to argue against your position first

Counter confirmation bias by explicitly asking it to attack your position. "Before giving your recommendation, argue the strongest possible case against this." This one technique prevents more bad decisions than any other.

5. Break complex tasks into sequential prompts

One prompt doing 5 things produces mediocre results on all 5. First prompt for research, second for analysis, third for writing. This is what the Playbooks section does.

6. Include examples of what good looks like

"Write a board memo in the style of the attached example, following the same structure and level of detail" produces work that matches your standards. Paste real examples when you have them.

7. Constrain length aggressively

"Maximum 150 words" or "This must fit on one page" forces the AI to prioritize — exactly what executive communication requires.

8. Use "before answering" to improve reasoning

"Before answering, identify what assumptions you are making." This forces the AI to think more carefully before committing to a response.

9. Iterate — the first output is your starting point

"Make this more direct." "Now make it 30% shorter." "Add specific data." Treat AI like a talented analyst who needs your editorial direction.

10. Specificity beats cleverness every time

Elaborate techniques matter less than being specific about what you want. A detailed, clear prompt beats any amount of prompt engineering jargon.

The 5 Biggest Mistakes Executives Make with AI

1Using AI to confirm what they already think

You ask leading questions and the AI agrees. Confirmation bias, automated.

Fix: Always include "Argue against this position first" or "What am I missing?" Force disagreement before agreement.

2Treating AI output as fact without verification

AI can generate plausible-sounding information that is wrong — especially specific numbers, dates, and legal citations.

Fix: Never cite AI-generated statistics without verification. Ask: "Rate your confidence 1-10 and explain what is uncertain."

3Putting sensitive data into consumer AI tools

Pasting client names, account numbers, or internal strategy documents into consumer AI creates regulatory, legal, and competitive risk.

Fix: Use anonymized data. Replace names with "Client A." Use enterprise AI tools for sensitive work. Never paste MNPI.

4Using one generic prompt when they need a specific one

"Help me prepare for this meeting" gives generic output. Specificity is everything.

Fix: Use the prompts in this dashboard — they are pre-built with the right specificity. Include: role, context, format, audience, and constraints.

5Not iterating — accepting the first response

The first AI output is a rough draft, not a final product. Most executives accept it as-is.

Fix: Plan for 2-3 rounds. First response → "Make it shorter" → "Strengthen the opening" → done. 2 extra minutes doubles the quality.

Tips for Getting the Best Results

  4. Start with 2-min prompts. Build confidence before tackling 30-min deep work sessions.
  5. One prompt, one job. Use playbooks to break complex tasks into sequential steps.
  6. Provide context generously. Who you are, who the audience is, what you've tried, what format you want.
  7. Magic phrases are universal. Append any magic phrase to any prompt — they work as add-ons.
  8. Privacy matters. Never paste confidential data or PII into consumer AI tools. Use anonymized inputs.
