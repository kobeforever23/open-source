Scenario Narratives & Stress Transmission
Each scenario represents a distinct macro regime with unique correlation structures, stress transmission channels, and portfolio impact profiles. The five scenarios span the ~65th to ~90th severity percentile, positioned below DFAST Severely Adverse (~95th+).

BAU Moderate Rates Rally

~65–70th percentile
Analog: 2019 mid-cycle easing + 2014 disinflation

A disinflationary slowdown driven by inflation undershooting Fed expectations as tariff effects prove transient and goods deflation resumes. Consumer spending decelerates but does not collapse. The labor market softens with unemployment rising to ~4.8–5.0% without severe deterioration. AI capex continues at a reduced pace. The Fed cuts aggressively, bringing the Fed funds rate to 2.00% as inflation drops toward 1.5–1.8%.

Growth slows to 0.5–1.0% — a slowdown, not a recession. Credit spreads widen only modestly (~52% of Moderate credit shocks). The primary stress vector is NII compression, reinvestment risk, and duration/convexity risk, particularly for MBS portfolios facing accelerated prepayments as mortgage rates decline.

BAU CCAR Firm Moderate

~70–75th percentile
Analog: 2015–16 commodity downturn + moderate recession

A broad-based cyclical downturn triggered by a convergence of: (a) consumer spending deceleration driven by eroding savings and credit deterioration; (b) AI capex retrenchment as ROI concerns trigger a 30–40% decline in technology infrastructure investment; (c) tariff-driven supply chain disruption and input cost inflation; (d) commercial real estate stress deepening in office and retail sectors; and (e) a moderate energy supply disruption (partial, transient Hormuz closure).

The Fed responds with 150–200 bp of rate cuts over the scenario horizon. Credit reprices from historically tight levels to stress levels consistent with prior moderate recessions. Equities decline 25%. Unemployment rises to 5.5–6.0%. No systemic financial crisis, but a meaningful correction in risk appetite and an acceleration of existing consumer/CRE credit deterioration trends.

BAU Moderate Flight to Quality

~70–75th percentile
Analog: March 2020 COVID liquidity crisis, 2011 EU debt crisis, 2023 SVB

An acute risk-off event driven by geopolitical escalation (Taiwan Strait tension, European banking stress), a major credit event (private credit fund gating, EM sovereign default scare), or a sudden loss of market confidence. Safe-haven flows pour into USTs, gold, and the USD. Credit spreads widen sharply — not because fundamentals deteriorate, but because liquidity evaporates and risk appetite collapses.

The episode is short-duration (1–3 months acute phase) but mark-to-market impact is severe. Funding markets seize: commercial paper issuance freezes (A1/P1 +200 bp), repo spreads widen (+28 bp), money market funds experience outflows. EM suffers capital flight (-15% FX). The key distinction from the Moderate: this is a liquidity event, not a fundamentals event. Oil falls (demand destruction fears), USD strengthens sharply (safe haven), and the negative stock-bond correlation re-emerges powerfully.

BAU Severe Rates Rally

~85–90th percentile
Analog: 2008–09 recession (severity) + 2020 COVID emergency cuts (speed)

A deep recession driven by consumer spending collapse as labor market deterioration accelerates (unemployment 7–8%), AI capex unwind amplifying GDP decline (-2 to -3%), CRE stress intensifying, and deflation risk emerging as demand destruction overwhelms supply-side inflation. The Fed cuts to near-zero emergency levels (0.75%), deploying QE and emergency lending facilities. Inflation drops below 2% and approaches deflation risk.

Credit spreads widen severely (recession-driven defaults), though less than in Severe Stagflation (no stagflation multiplier from energy). THE DEFINING FEATURE is the massive MBS prepayment wave: mortgage rates fall to ~2.5%, triggering refi activity that overwhelms servicer capacity. CPR surges from 9 to 40–50, collapsing MBS effective duration from ~5.2Y to 2.5–3.0Y — a devastating negative convexity event for portfolios that are long MBS duration. Oil collapses (-30%) on demand destruction. Gold surges (+25%) on real rate collapse.

BAU Severe Stagflation

~90th percentile
Analog: 1973–74 oil crisis + stagflation

A severe stagflationary episode driven by a sustained Hormuz closure keeping oil at $100–115/bbl, tariff escalation pushing core goods inflation toward 3%, consumer credit stress cascading from subprime auto and credit cards into broader consumption weakness, CRE loan defaults accelerating with office CMBS delinquency exceeding 20%, AI capex collapse creating a technology-sector recession, and EM capital flight amplifying global financial stress.

Oil remains elevated, driving CPI above 4%. The Fed is constrained at 4.00% — unable to cut (inflation) or hike (growth). 10Y UST rises +138 bp to 5.50% as the market prices in persistent inflation. Credit spreads widen dramatically from compressed levels. Equities decline 40%. The defining MBS dynamic is the mirror of Severe Rates Rally: duration extension from ~5.2Y to 7.0–7.5Y as prepayments collapse (CPR drops to 5–6), amplifying mark-to-market losses in rising rates. The twin-deficit narrative (fiscal + current account) undermines USD confidence (-3%).
