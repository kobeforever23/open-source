Regulatory posture - what he will care about Regulatory posture: U.S. banking supervisors have not created a separate AI exception regime. Their message has been: existing legal, conduct, technology, third-party, and model-risk frameworks still apply; AI just makes disciplined implementation harder and more important. (cid:127) SR 11-7 remains the anchor. It applies to models used for identifying and measuring risk, valuing exposures, stress testing, capital assessment, internal limits, and regulatory reporting. [S15] (cid:127) The OCC's updated Model Risk Management handbook explicitly contemplates AI and machine learning use cases in banks, and the OCC's Spring 2025 Semiannual Risk Perspective says GenAI can help in enterprise risk management and data analysis but can also introduce model, cybersecurity, and compliance risks. [S16][S17] (cid:127) Barr said in April 2025 that banks using GenAI must manage the risk, demand transparency from fintech partners, and pay close attention to data quality, training, and bias; in November 2025 he added that GenAI used in financial models must be well controlled, numerically and legally precise, explainable, and replicable. [S18][S19] (cid:127) Bowman likewise argued that AI does not sit outside the existing legal and regulatory framework, citing fair lending, cybersecurity, data privacy, and third-party risk management. [S21] (cid:127) Treasury's December 2024 AI-in-financial-services report and the sector risk plan that followed make clear that policymakers are focusing heavily on operational risk, cyber risk, fraud, cloud concentration, and resilience. [S22][S23A] Your crisp regulatory take The correct posture is: use AI to accelerate analysis, synthesis, and workflow orchestration around validated risk processes; be much more cautious about letting LLMs drive the numerical engines themselves. That is not a formal regulatory quote - it is the practical inference from SR 11-7, OCC guidance, and recent Fed speeches. AI-generated severe scenario design I built an AI-assisted severe stress scenario from scratch with mathematically derived equity shocks, cross-asset credit overlays, and volatility-surface calibration. What mattered was not that AI 'picked' the scenario; it accelerated the iteration loop so I could get to a coherent, defendable shock architecture much faster. The lesson was that AI is strongest as a structured design partner, not as a substitute for market judgment. 2. Python automation for core risk workflows I used AI to accelerate development of Python tooling for stress P&L; attribution, VaR explain, and driver-level decomposition, and it materially compressed build time. The real value was not just speed; it was being able to prototype, test, and refine analytics much more aggressively. I learned that AI's highest ROI shows up in scoped technical workflows where the data logic is precise and the human can validate fast. 3. Scenario generation wizard with governance I designed a stress-scenario generation wizard concept with embedded governance and approval workflows so scenario creation could scale across desks without becoming a free-for-all. That matters because in risk, generation is only half the problem; the other half is traceability, challenge, approval, and repeatability. What I learned is that the best AI products in banking are workflow-and-control systems, not just model wrappers. 4. CRO-level intelligence briefs I have used AI to assemble CRO-level intelligence briefs that combine live market context, geopolitical developments, and cross-asset contagion mapping - including Iran-related scenarios - in hours rather than weeks. That matters because senior leaders do not need more data; they need compressed signal and clean structure under time pressure. The lesson was that retrieval, synthesis, and editorial discipline are where LLMs become institutionally useful. 5. Interactive risk visualization I built interactive HTML dashboards with a Bloomberg Terminal aesthetic for internal risk visualization. That sounds cosmetic, but it is not - adoption goes up when the output feels native to how decision-makers already process information. I learned that AI in risk is partly a UX problem: insight only matters if the consumer can absorb it quickly. 6. Build-vs-buy perspective with technical grounding I have been evaluating Rogo versus our internal Tachyon path while also finishing Penn's MCIT part-time, so I think about this from both a workflow and systems perspective. The key takeaway for me is that frontier models will keep improving and commoditizing; the durable edge for a bank will be proprietary data, entitlements, orchestration, control design, and how tightly the tooling maps to actual desk workflows. AI / CIB RISK ANALYTICS / CONVERSATION PREP WELLS FARGO CIB 4 TEN KILLER IDEAS AND USE CASES These are intentionally not 'automate reports' ideas. Each is meant to make a senior risk leader pause and think. 1. News-to-scenario compiler Idea: Build an AI layer that ingests major news, policy, and geopolitics flows and proposes desk-specific shock vectors, historical analogs, and likely second-order contagion channels. Value: This compresses time-to-scenario when markets move faster than committee cycles, especially in cross-asset events where narrative travels before clean market consensus exists. Feasibility: Feasible now if grounded in trusted market data, internal exposure maps, and human approval; the AI should generate candidate scenarios, not authorize them. 2. LLM-powered limit breach triage Idea: When a desk breaches a limit, have an agent automatically classify the driver, compare it with historical breach episodes, identify likely benign versus structural causes, and draft the first escalation pack. Value: Value comes from speed, consistency, and freeing senior analysts for judgment rather than repetitive assembly. Feasibility: Feasible in the near term because the workflow is document-heavy, citation-friendly, and can remain fully human-approved. 3. Scenario coverage gap mapper Idea: Embed the existing stress library and compare it against live portfolio exposures to identify where current scenarios under-cover concentrations, basis risk, or new product structures. Value: This upgrades scenario design from periodic craftsmanship to continuous coverage management. Feasibility: Feasible if you can access position metadata, risk-factor mappings, and the scenario inventory in a clean retrieval layer. 4. VaR explain hypothesis engine Idea: When VaR explain looks odd, let an agent run a structured hypothesis tree across new trades, vol moves, curve shape changes, basis shifts, and mapping anomalies before handing the analyst a ranked explanation set. Value: This reduces analyst time spent on brute-force debugging and can shorten the latency between anomaly detection and credible root cause. Feasibility: Feasible if the system is constrained to controlled internal data sources and produces evidence-backed hypotheses rather than final answers. 5. Desk-level risk appetite calibration Idea: Use AI to compare approved risk appetite versus realized risk-taking, P&L; efficiency, stress losses, and escalation frequency by desk, then suggest where limits may be too loose, too tight, or poorly designed. Value: This turns AI from a reporting aid into a decision-quality tool for management governance. Feasibility: Feasible as an analytics co-pilot, not an autonomous recommender; the output should be framed as challenge material for leadership. AI / CIB RISK ANALYTICS / CONVERSATION PREP WELLS FARGO CIB 4 TEN KILLER IDEAS AND USE CASES (CONT.) 6. Generative stress narratives for regulators and senior committees Idea: Create a system that can draft scenario narratives, methodology notes, and impact summaries linked to actual data lineage and prior governance artifacts. Value: This is valuable because the bottleneck in high-stakes stress work is often explanatory packaging rather than numeric computation. Feasibility: Feasible today if every claim is retrieval-grounded and the workflow keeps an analyst and approver in the loop. 7. Cross-desk contagion graph Idea: Build an AI-assisted graph of hidden linkages across desks - shared issuers, funding channels, hedge proxies, volatility regimes, and wrong-way risk patterns - to surface where a localized shock could travel non-linearly. Value: Senior management cares about where risks stop being siloed. This helps convert desk views into enterprise views. Feasibility: Feasible if you start narrow around one asset class or event family and use AI primarily for entity resolution and narrative synthesis. 8. Model-change radar Idea: Create an agent that scans market-structure changes, benchmark reforms, new product features, vendor methodology changes, and internal model changes to flag models or assumptions that may need review. Value: This shifts model risk from reactive validation to proactive surveillance. Feasibility: Feasible because the challenge is mostly information monitoring, relevance ranking, and workflow routing. 9. Unstructured document risk extraction Idea: Use AI to parse term sheets, structured notes, muni disclosures, warehouse agreements, or securitization documents to identify embedded optionality, triggers, covenants, or basis exposures that standard fields miss. Value: The value is in surfacing hidden risk mechanics before they become 'surprises' in stress or P&L; explain. Feasibility: Feasible for bounded document sets with strong citation requirements; less feasible for fully autonomous interpretation. 10. AI-assisted scenario challenge function Idea: Instead of generating scenarios only, build an adversarial challenger that attacks an existing scenario set: what is missing, what is too consensus, what assumptions are inconsistent, and which portfolios it fails to meaningfully stress. Value: This is clever because it uses AI to improve challenge quality, not just production speed. Feasibility: Feasible now if you treat the model as a structured critic and require analysts to accept or reject each challenge with written rationale. The point is not to get through all of them; the point is to ask one or two that move the discussion up a level. Where in the CIB risk stack do you think AI can add the most value first: upstream in intelligence and triage, midstream in scenario design and explain, or downstream in governance and communication? How are you thinking about the boundary between AI that augments analyst judgment and AI that materially influences a numerical risk decision under SR 11-7 expectations? In your view, what is the real bottleneck to scaling AI in risk at a large bank today - model capability, data architecture, governance throughput, or management attention? Do you think the durable edge for banks will come from proprietary internal platforms, selective vendor adoption, or a hybrid architecture where internal control layers sit on top of external model capabilities? If you were funding one AI pilot in CIB risk that had to prove meaningful value in 90 days, what problem would you put chips behind and what metric would you judge it on? How do you think about explainability in practice for LLM-enabled workflows: is the right standard full model interpretability, or is it process transparency, citation quality, and reproducible outputs? What capability do you think risk analytics teams will need most over the next two years that traditional quant or reporting skill sets do not fully cover today? Best sequencing Start with a strategic architecture question. Then, once he gives you his view, connect it back to your own work. That creates a genuine exchange rather than a quiz.
